⚙How to drastically cut OPEX

Same playbook regardless of scenario. The cheaper we can run, the longer our runway and the lower the deal-volume we need to break even.

  • Free tiers, not paid: Cloudflare Workers, Supabase, GitHub, Resend, Vercel — all have free tiers that cover <$50k revenue projects easily.
  • Sole proprietor before LLC: NY LLC formation is $200 filing + $200-1500 mandatory publication. Skip until revenue justifies it.
  • Insurance only when required: E&O policies start ~$1500/yr. Don't buy until a customer's contract requires it OR we're handling regulated data.
  • One template, lawyer-reviewed once: A $500 lawyer review of an engagement letter template amortizes across every project. Don't pay a lawyer per deal.
  • Skip SOC 2 until enterprise: Vanta/Drata cost $7-15k/yr before the auditor. Useless until we're chasing customers who require it.
  • 1099 contractors, not W-2 employees: Until $500k+ run-rate, hiring 1099s avoids payroll tax, benefits, unemployment, workers' comp.
  • No office. No conferences. No ads. Coffee shops, Zoom, warm-lead referrals. Marketing dollars are the easiest to defer.
  • Personal devices, personal subscriptions: Doug + Jon already own the hardware + Claude/1Password/etc. Don't double-buy under a business name.
  • Tax structure: pass-through. Sole prop or single-member LLC means business income flows to personal returns. No double taxation, no separate corporate filing fees.

Anything that's "nice to have for legitimacy" but doesn't directly produce revenue → defer.

Personal-garage math: almost every "running a software side-business" cost is either free (free tier of every modern dev tool), already-sunk (1Password / personal Claude / personal devices), or genuinely not legally required (E&O, LLC, SOC 2). The honest floor is one $25-35 DBA filing if you want the business name, otherwise truly $0.
What we explicitly DON'T spend money on: LLC formation ($200-1700 in NY, skipped — sole props don't need it), E&O insurance ($1500-3000/yr, skipped — not legally required), lawyer-reviewed MSA ($500-1000 one-time, skipped — DIY template is fine for friend-rate work), accounting software ($240+/yr, skipped — a spreadsheet handles 5-10 invoices/year), conferences / marketing / ads ($0, warm referrals only). All of these become "real business" line items at the Small Biz tier; they don't belong in Garage mode.

Time commitment

3-5 hours/week each. Truly side project. Doug works on this on weekends, Jon contributes evenings as needed.

What we DON'T do at this tier

  • No LLC. Both contract as sole proprietors. 1099 income flows to personal taxes.
  • No insurance. Don't take engagements that require it.
  • No formal contracts beyond a one-page engagement letter (template, copy-pasted).
  • No outbound sales. Warm referrals only. If we run out of warm leads we slow down, not pay for ads.
  • No SOC 2 / HIPAA / regulated-data work.
  • No marketing spend. Public site exists; it doesn't get pushed.

Pros / cons

Pros: Effectively zero risk. Doesn't compete with day jobs. Quick to start (zero setup). Easy to walk away from if life gets busy.

Cons: Won't grow on its own. If demand turns out to be strong, we leave money on the table. Can't take regulated-data customers. Personal liability exposure if something goes wrong (mitigated by careful client selection).

Trigger to graduate to Scenario B: 5+ paying customers in 12 months. 2+ asking for more work or sending referrals. Pipeline we're turning away.

★Doug's recommendation

Start at A. Pick up 2-4 warm-lead projects. Spend $0-35 total — literally one DBA filing if we want the "Hudson Valley AI" name, otherwise just operate as our legal names. See if we actually like working together on something with stakes (vs. just talking about it). See if customers come back.

If A delivers, B is the natural next step in year 2. We don't go to C until we've earned it with year-2 numbers.

The reason this stacking matters: C only works if customers think we're worth $1M of their business. The way they come to think that is through A and B delivering for them. Skipping to C without that track record is the most common reason consultancies fail — they burn $180k OPEX before they've proven the offer works.

Bottom line: A is the right starting answer. B is the right destination for year 2. C is the right destination for year 3-4 if everything goes well.

Three scenarios. One choice. We can always level up later — we can't easily level down once we've committed.
