①How to use this

50 real Westchester businesses sorted by how well they fit our offer + DecisionLayer + skateOS pattern-match. Researched via web search; everything is verifiable except items flagged [verify]. Sort order within each tier is roughly by fit score (5 = warmest).

  • Score 5 (green): Strong fit on multiple dimensions — software pain + waiver / contract heavy + owner-led + Doug has analogous pattern shipping.
  • Score 4 (amber): Strong fit on most dimensions, smaller deal size or less obvious DecisionLayer angle.
  • Score 3 (gray): Worth a call but lower priority — possibly franchise-affected decision-making, or fit is more "general dev shop" than "us specifically."
One golden rule: only cold-outreach 5-10 in the first month. Quality > quantity. Two of these turning into a Spike is a great month.

Recommended cadence: 5 emails / 3 in-person walk-ins / 2 phone calls per week. Track every touch (a Notion or Google Sheet is fine).

Why this one over all 49 others

Climbing gyms are the cleanest possible ICP intersection. Doug already runs skateOS for a comparable action-sport facility (waivers, memberships, punch cards, lessons, parties, minors with guardians). The Rock Club is independent, single-location, owner-supervised, established 2006 — exactly the size where one good software conversation reaches the decision-maker in one phone call.

Business profile

  • Parent umbrella: Pine Brook Fitness — also operates The New Rochelle Racquet Club + "The Studios." This is a small multi-facility independent operator, not a chain.
  • Revenue lines: memberships, day passes, coaching, kids' after-school programs, summer climbing camps, birthday parties, bar/bat mitzvahs, corporate events, fundraisers. Heavy minor-climber volume = heavy guardian-waiver volume.
  • Public management contact: Jordan P. listed as manager. Owner not publicly named — 5-min LinkedIn lookup before calling (search "Pine Brook Fitness owner" + Westchester biz registry).

Their likely software stack

Industry standard for an indie climbing gym this size is Rock Gym Pro (RGP). Recurring complaints from public reviews — these are gold for the cold-call:

  1. Data loss on waivers and belay certifications — customers forced to redo waivers. This is exactly what DecisionLayer + persistent intake fixes.
  2. Group booking is a nightmare — multiple windows, "least intuitive aspect," painful when groups arrive without exact headcount. A 17,000 sqft gym hosting parties every weekend lives this pain.
  3. System downtime + constant re-login.
  4. Steep learning curve for front-desk hires.
  5. No native Mac client — long-standing user request.

If they're not on RGP, the next-most-likely stack is Square POS + Mindbody + paper / DocuSign waivers. Either way the pain points are predictable.

Why DecisionLayer hits especially hard for them

A climbing gym signs liability waivers on every walk-in and every minor. Every dispute that escalates — bruised kid, dropped belay, equipment damage claim — is exactly the use case DecisionLayer is built for. The pitch isn't "AI is cool"; it's:

"Your waiver already exists. We're adding one clause that routes disputes to a structured AI-mediated review before they become lawsuits or insurance claims. Insurers love it. Plaintiffs' attorneys hate it. It costs you nothing on day one and lowers your premium conversation."

The first-30-seconds homework question

"How many belay certifications has Rock Gym Pro lost on you in the last twelve months — and what does your front desk do when a regular shows up and the system says they were never certified?"

This question signals (a) we know their stack, (b) we know the specific failure mode the community complains about, (c) we're not selling generic software — we're solving a problem they've already lived. It's a 30-second test of whether they'll lean in.

What to bring to the first meeting

A 5-minute screen-share of skateOS's live waiver-resolution flow at 2nd Nature Park: front desk types a climber's name → if waiver is missing/expired the system blocks check-in and offers four resolution paths (paper-confirmed, emailed Smartwaiver link, texted SMS link, in-person tablet sign). Plus a one-page mock of a Rock Club-branded liability waiver with the DecisionLayer arbitration clause inserted as paragraph 6 — show, don't tell.

Goal of meeting 1: walk out with verbal interest in a $5k Spike to prototype a Rock Club-shaped version. No closing pressure.

If The Rock Club doesn't bite within 2 weeks: the same playbook works on Air Riderz Port Chester (trampoline park, A3), Crazy Paint (paintball, A7), Old Salem Farm (equestrian, A10), or Camp Hillard (day camp, E1). The pitch flexes — waivers + minors + parties + party-bookings is the through-line.

②Outreach playbook

Same shape for every business on this list. Adapt the language; don't adapt the structure.

  1. 5-min homework: open their site, find a software pain (e.g., booking form looks like 2008, "call to schedule"), find owner's name on LinkedIn / About page.
  2. First touch — email. 3 sentences max. Reference their specific pain. Offer 20-min meeting. Sign off with phone number.
  3. No reply in 5 days: walk-in OR call. "Hi, did Doug Brown's email land in your inbox last week?"
  4. Meeting: 20 min. Show a real demo (skateOS waiver flow). Ask 3 questions about their pain. Close on a $5k Spike, not a $20k Sprint.
  5. Follow-up: within 24 hours, email a one-page scope. Decision in 1 week or pass to next prospect.
Track everything. A simple Google Sheet with columns: business / first-touch-date / response / next-step / status (cold / warm / meeting-booked / proposed / closed / lost). Don't fancy-tool this in month 1.
